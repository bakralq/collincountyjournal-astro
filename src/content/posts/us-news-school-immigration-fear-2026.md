A school does not need an immigration raid at the front door to feel the pressure of immigration enforcement.

Sometimes the first sign is quieter than that: a missing application, an empty seat, a parent who stops coming to campus, or a child who starts missing class because the route to school no longer feels simple or safe.

That is the pattern now starting to show itself more clearly in parts of the country, and it matters because it changes school systems before many people even realize the shift is underway.

The clearest recent example came out of Washington, D.C. The Washington Post reported on April 12 that applications through the city’s public school lottery fell sharply for the 2026 to 2027 school year, especially in immigrant neighborhoods and Spanish-dominant dual-language programs. Pre-K applications dropped 14%, and applications to Spanish-dominant dual-language schools fell nearly 25%. The Post reported that deportation fears and visible enforcement pressure were part of the reason.

## Quick Read

- The Washington Post reported a 14% drop in D.C. pre-K applications and nearly a 25% drop in Spanish-dominant dual-language school applications for 2026 to 2027.
- Brookings reported this week that stronger immigration enforcement and the rescinding of protections for schools, churches, and hospitals have destabilized education for children from immigrant families.
- Brookings said research links heightened enforcement to chronic absenteeism, lower test scores, poorer mental health, and reduced college enrollment, especially for Latino and English learner students.
- The Texas Tribune reported on April 2 that sweeping recent changes in Texas have cut undocumented immigrants off from school, work, and driving, disrupting daily life for undocumented people and some noncitizens who are here legally.

## The Pattern Behind It

The force driving this story is not ideology. It is withdrawal.

When families begin to see school routines through the lens of enforcement risk, the damage starts before there is any official closure, any dramatic campus incident, or any major district announcement. The school loses something first that is harder to measure and harder to restore: confidence.

Brookings put that directly this week, arguing that increasingly aggressive immigration enforcement, combined with the loss of protections for places like schools, churches, and hospitals, has destabilized the education of children from immigrant families. The research it cited ties heightened enforcement to chronic absenteeism, poorer mental health, lower academic performance, and weaker college pathways.

## Why The D.C. Example Matters

That is what makes the D.C. numbers more than a local oddity. They are a visible sign of something broader.

And Texas is not insulated from that broader pressure. The Texas Tribune reported on April 2 that over the last year, Texas has enacted legal and regulatory changes that have upended daily life for noncitizens, including access connected to school, work, and driving. That matters because families do not experience these systems one at a time. They experience them all at once.

Transportation, employment, documentation, school attendance, and fear of exposure can all converge into a single decision: stay home.

## Why It Matters Here

For Collin County, that matters even if no district is publicly reporting a crisis.

Prosper, Celina, Anna, Melissa, and Princeton are part of a fast-growing suburban region where schools depend on stable attendance patterns, predictable enrollment, strong parent trust, and long-range planning. If even a slice of families begin changing behavior because school travel, school paperwork, or school visibility feels riskier than before, districts can feel the effects in ways that reach far beyond politics.

Attendance gets less predictable. Parent engagement weakens. Student stress rises. Program demand shifts. Planning gets harder.

## Bottom Line

This is not only an immigration story. It is an education story.

It is about whether schools remain places families feel safe enough to use normally. For local readers, the question now is not whether every district is already seeing this. It is whether districts are ready if they do.
