New stores do not end the question.

They change it.

Celina has Lowe's open on South Preston Road. Walmart opens April 29. Home Depot is moving through construction. Costco is being tracked toward late 2026. Restaurants and service tenants are filling in along Preston and Frontier.

So the useful question now is different.

What do residents still leave Celina for?

[Celina Watch](https://celinawatch.com/the-blueprint/issue-002/) recently gave one public signal. In a poll asking what food concept Celina was missing, 54 responses were split between sushi at 28 percent, seafood at 26 percent, steakhouse at 26 percent, and Tex-Mex at 20 percent.

The dominant pattern is gap shopping.

Residents may get more local basics and still leave town for specific experiences.

## Quick Read

- Celina's retail base is expanding quickly, but gaps remain.
- Celina Watch polling showed sushi, seafood, steakhouse, and Tex-Mex all drawing interest as missing food concepts.
- Lowe's is already operating in Celina.
- Walmart opens April 29.
- Home Depot, Costco, and other projects are being tracked through construction or site work.
- The next useful question is what Celina still lacks, not whether growth is happening.
- The clearest gap categories are dining, specialty grocery, medical care, entertainment, kids activities, fitness, furniture, and late-night options.

## The Retail Shift

Celina's old retail problem was scarcity.

Residents had to leave for too many everyday needs.

The new retail problem is specificity.

Walmart can solve many basic errands. Lowe's already gives the city a major home-improvement anchor. Home Depot would add a second. Costco can solve bulk retail.

But those stores do not answer every daily-life need.

Residents still evaluate a city through what they have to drive for on a Friday night, during a medical need, after school, or when guests come into town.

## What The Poll Shows

The Celina Watch food poll is small, but useful.

Sushi, seafood, steakhouse, and Tex-Mex were all close.

That tells us residents are not asking only for fast food.

They are asking for sit-down options, date-night options, and food categories that make a city feel more complete.

The spread also matters.

No single category dominated. That suggests Celina's gap is broader than one restaurant type.

## What Big-Box Retail Does Not Solve

Major retail anchors help a city.

They do not replace a downtown dining scene, a hospital, specialty medical offices, youth recreation, live entertainment, boutique shopping, independent coffee, or late-night options.

Those categories often arrive later because they need population, traffic, rooftops, disposable income, and the right sites.

That is why residents can be excited about Walmart and still say they leave Celina for dinner, health care, entertainment, or shopping that feels less routine.

## The Real Test

The real test is where residents still spend money outside the city.

If the most common trips are for restaurants, medical care, youth activities, fitness, clothing, furniture, or late-night options, then Celina's retail growth is still incomplete.

That does not mean the new anchors failed.

It means they solved one layer of demand while revealing the next one.

## Why This Matters

Retail demand is not only a private market issue.

It affects sales tax, traffic, land use, quality of life, and whether residents spend money locally or somewhere else.

Every dollar that leaves Celina for Frisco, Prosper, McKinney, Plano, or Denton is a clue.

It tells developers and city leaders where the local market still feels incomplete.

## Bottom Line

Celina is no longer waiting for growth to show up.

It is deciding what kind of daily-life city it becomes after the anchors arrive.

The next stage will be measured less by whether big stores open and more by whether residents still feel they have to leave town for the things that make a place feel finished.
